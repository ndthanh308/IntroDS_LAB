\keywords{\fr manifolds, sprays, connection maps, symmetric linear connections}

\begin{abstract}
	For \fr manifolds by using sprays, we construct connection maps, linear symmetric connections on tangent  and second-order tangent bundles. We characterize linear symmetric  connections on tangent bundles by using the bilinear symmetric mappings associated with a given spray on a manifold. Moreover, we give another characterization of linear symmetric connections on tangent bundles using tangent structures. We prove that there is a bijective correspondence between linear symmetric connections on tangent bundles and  connection maps induced by  sprays on a manifold.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
The geometry of \fr manifolds, in particular higher order structures on tangent bundles, has progressed widely in the past few years, see for example \cite{a,a2,a3, a5,dod,dod2,dod3,dod4,su1}.
A crucial concern in dealing with these manifolds is that the general linear group of a \fr space is not a Lie group, therefore, it can not serve as a structure group of bundles. Another concern is that the space of continuous linear mappings between \fr spaces does not admit a \fr space structure, therefore, the definitions of structures  that involve this space is not a trivial generalization of the Banach case.

An idea to resolve these problems is the projective limit technique used in the mentioned papers. 
In this approach, we consider those \fr manifolds which are the projective limits of Banach manifolds so that objects can be constructed using projective limits of their Banach counterparts.  
This approach has been successful in obtaining many valuable results that are available in the above papers. However, in situations where we do not need a structure group a question that arises is whether there are other ways to resolve these problems. Because in the projective limit approach, we need to construct all objects through projective limit systems which is not always easy and obtainable. In  this work, we will try to set up an alternative approach without using general linear groups to construct geometrical objects using sprays which have not been studied much on \fr manifolds.     

Despite the limitations, even if we want to define differentiable bundles on \fr manifolds  without the use of a structure group, we face this question: how to define the differentiability of transition mappings?
 (because they take their values in a general linear group). In \cite{hamilton}, Hamilton  provided such a definition. Another definition was given by Neeb \cite{neeb}. In this work, we will apply the latter definition which seems to be more applicable to develop the geometry of \fr manifolds. First, we show that a given spray determines a unique  connection map (a notion due to Dombrowski \cite{dom}), Theorem \ref{th:con}. This is a key result that links  a spray with other objects such as connections on tangent and second-order tangent bundles. It is customary to use a parallel transport to define a nonlinear connection. However, this is not the case for \fr manifolds because parallel transports do not necessarily exist. We will use directly a spray to construct on tangent bundles symmetric linear connections  in Theorem \ref{th:ch},  and via a tangent structure in 
 Theorem \ref{th:noncon}. Also, we construct linear symmetric connections on second-order tangent bundles in Theorem \ref{th:ind}.
 In Theorem \ref{th:ch}, we 
 characterize a linear symmetric connection by using symmetric linear mappings associated with a given spray. In Theorem \ref{th:ind}, we show that any linear symmetric connection on $ T\fs{M} $ induces a linear symmetric connection on the vector bundle $ T^2\fs{M} $, and vice versa. Moreover, by using the notion of a tangent structure that has a direct relation with  connection maps, we prove the  coexistence of connection maps (induced by a spray) and linear symmetric connections on tangent bundles in Theorem \ref{th:noncon}. 
 
 For \fr manifolds, as we will see sprays and group structureless vector bundles provide a neat and accessible way to 
 study geometrical objects on tangent and second-order tangent bundles. We even see many similarities with the geometry of Banach manifolds. There are further important topics that with this point of view can be studied, for example,  second order differential equations on the base \fr manifold which is the configuration space  of a physical filed.    
\section{Prerequisites}\label{sec:1}
We will apply the following notion of differentiable mappings  known as $C^k$-maps in the 
Michal--Bastiani sense, or Keller's $C_c^k$-maps, because it does not involve any topology on spaces 
of continuous linear maps. 

\begin{defn}[\cite{neeb}, Definition I.2.1]
Let $\fs{E}$ and $\fs{F}$ be locally convex spaces, $ U \opn \fs{E} $ an open subspace, and $\va: U  \to  \fs{F}$ a mapping. Then the  derivative
	of $\va$ at $x$ in the direction $h$ is defined as 
$$ \dd\va(x)(h) := \derat0 \va(x + t h) 
= \lim_{t \to 0} {1\over t}(\va(x+th) -\va(x)) $$
whenever it exists. The function $\va$ is called differentiable at
	$x$ if $\dd \va(x)(h)$ exists for all $h \in \fs{E}$. It is called 
	continuously differentiable, if it is differentiable at all
points of $U$ and 
$$ \dd \va : U \times \fs{E} \to \fs{F}, \quad (x,h) \mapsto \dd \va(x)(h) $$
is a continuous map. It is called a {\it $C^k$-map}, $k \in \nn \cup \{\infty\}$, 
if it is continuous, the iterated directional derivatives 
$ \dd^{j}\va(x)(h_1,\ldots, h_j)
$
exist for all integers $j \leq k$, $x \in U$ and $h_1,\ldots, h_j \in \fs{E}$, 
and all maps $\dd^j \va : U \times \fs{E}^j \to F$ are continuous. We call $ C^{\infty} $-maps smooth. 
\end{defn}
\begin{defn}[\cite{neeb}, Definition II.3.1]\label{def:neeb}
	Let $ \fs{M} $ be a $ C^k,\, (k\geq 1) $ locally convex manifold, and $ \mathrm{Diff}(\fs{M}) $ the group of
	diffeomorphisms of $ \fs{M} $. Further, let $ \fs{N} $ be a $ C^k$ locally convex manifold. Although, in general, $ \mathrm{Diff}(\fs{M} ) $ has
	no natural Lie group structure,  a map $ \va: \fs{N} \to \mathrm{Diff}(\fs{M})$ is said to be $ C^k$, if the following map 	is of class $ C^k$:
	\begin{equation*}
	\widehat{\va} : \fs{N} \times \fs{M} \to \fs{M} \times \fs{M}, \quad (n,x) \mapsto (\va(n)(x),\va^{-1}(n)(x))
	\end{equation*}
\end{defn}
\begin{defn}[\cite{neeb}, Definition I.3.8]\label{def:neebvec}
Let $\fs{M}$ be a $ C^k $-Fr\'{e}chet manifold modeled on a Fr\'{e}chet space $\fs{F}$, $ k\geq1 $, and $ \fs{E} $ another \fr space.	
 A $C^r$-{vector bundle} of type $\fs{E}$ over $\fs{M}$ is a triple $(\pr, \fs{V}, \fs{E})$, consisting of a $C^r$-manifold $ \fs{V} $, a $ C^r $-map $ \pr: \fs{V} \to \fs{M} $ and a \fr space $ \fs{E} $, with the following properties:
\begin{description}
	\item[(VB.1)]  $ \forall m \in \fs{M} $, the fiber $ \fs{V}_m := \pr^{-1}(m) $ is a \fr space 
	isomorphic to $\fs{E} $.
	\item[(VB.2)] Each  $ m \in \fs{M} $ has an open neighborhood $ U $ for which there exists a diffeomorphism
	\begin{equation*}
	\upphi_U:\pr^{-1}(U) \to U \times \fs{E} 
	\end{equation*}
	with $ \upphi_U = (\pr \rvert_U, \uppsi_U) $, where $ \uppsi_U  : \pr^{-1}(U) \to \fs{E} $ is linear on each  $ \fs{V}_m, m \in U$.		
\end{description}
We then call $ U $ a trivializing subset of $ \fs{M} $ and $ \upphi_U $ a bundle chart. If $ \upphi_U $ and $ \upphi_V $ are two bundle charts and $ U \cap V \neq \emptyset $, then we obtain a diffeomorphism
\begin{equation*}
\upphi_U \circ  \upphi_V^{-1}: (U \cap V) \times \fs{E} \to (U \cap V) \times \fs{E}
\end{equation*}
of the form $ (x, v) \mapsto (x, \uppsi_{VU} (x)v) $. This leads to a map
$ \uppsi_{UV} : U \cap V \to \bl{GL}(\fs{E}) $
for which it does not make sense to speak about smoothness because $ \bl{GL}(\fs{E}) $ is not a Lie group. Nevertheless, $ \uppsi_{UV} $ is of class $ C^r $ in the sense (Definition \ref{def:neeb}) that the map
\begin{gather*}
\widehat{\uppsi_{UV}}: (U \cap V) \times \fs{E} \to (U \cap V) \times \fs{E} \\
(x,v) \mapsto
(\uppsi_{UV}(x)v,\uppsi_{UV}(x)^{-1}v) =(\uppsi_{UV}(x)v,\uppsi_{VU}(x)v)
\end{gather*}
is of class $ C^r $. Here, $ \bl{GL}(\fs{E}) $ is the general linear group of $ \fs{E} $.
\end{defn}
Sprays were worked out for some category of \fr manifolds ($ \mc{k} $-\fr manifolds) in \cite{k3}.
The following definitions and statements will remain valid also for $ C^k $-\fr manifolds.
 
Henceforth, we assume that $\fs{M}$ is a $ C^k $-Fr\'{e}chet manifold modeled on a Fr\'{e}chet space $\fs{F}$, $ k\geq3 $, and $ \fs{M} $ admits a smooth partition of unity. 

We  denote by $ T\fs{M} $ the tangent bundle, and by  $ T\va  $  (or $ \va_{*} $) the tangent map of a map $ \va $ defined on $ \fs{M} $.  To denote fibers of bundles and restrictions of maps to fibers we use subscripts.

A second-order vector field $ \vf $ (of class $ C^k $) is a $ C^k $-mapping $\vf : T\fs{M} \to T(T\fs{M})$  such that
$$ T\pr_{\fs{M}} \circ \vf =\Id_{T\fs{M}}.$$ Let $ \mathbb{V}: \fs{N} \to \fs{M} $ be a $ C^k $-vector bundle,  $ s $ a fixed real number. Define the mapping: 
\begin{equation*}
L_{\mathbb{V}}: \fs{N} \to \fs{N},\quad v \mapsto sv.
\end{equation*}
Then, its tangent map $ TL_{\mathbb{V}}: T(\fs{N}) \to T(\fs{N}) $ is the  induced map on $ T(\fs{N}) $.
If $ \fs{N}= T\fs{M}  $, then
\begin{equation*}
(L_{T\fs{M}})_* \circ L_{T(T\fs{M})}= L_{T(T\fs{M})} \circ (L_{T\fs{M}})_*
\end{equation*}
which follows from the linearity of $ L_{T\fs{M}} $ on each fiber.

A second-order vector filed $\s : T\fs{M} \to T(T\fs{M})$ (of class $ C^{k-1} $) is said to be {spray} if 
\begin{enumerate}[label=$ \bl{(SP.\arabic*)} $,ref=SP.\arabic*]
	\item \label{eq:sp1} $\s(sv) = (L_{T\fs{M}})_*(s\s(v))$ for
	all $ s \in \rr $ and $ v \in T\fs{M} $.
\end{enumerate}
If $ \fs{M} $ admits a smooth partition of unity there exists a spray on $ \fs{M} $.

Let $ U \opn \fs{F} $, so that $ T(U) = U \times \fs{F} $, and
$ T(T(U)) = (U \times \fs{F} ) \times (\fs{F} \times \fs{F}) $. 
Then, the representations of $ L_{TU} $ and $ (L_{TU})_*$ in the chart are given by the
maps:
\begin{equation*}
L_{TU}:(x,v) \mapsto (x,sv) \quad \text{and}\quad (L_{TU})_*:(x,v,u,w)\mapsto (x,sv,u,sw).
\end{equation*}
Therefore, we get
\begin{equation*}
L_{T(TU)} \circ (L_{TU})_*(x,v,u,w)=(x,sv,su,s^2w).
\end{equation*}
In order to avoid confusion, if necessarily, in a chart $ U $, we index objects by $ U $ to show their local representations.
Let $ {\vf}_U= ({\vf}_{U,1},{\vf}_{U,2}): (U \times \fs{F} ) \to \fs{F} \times \fs{F}$
be a local representation of $ \vf $, each $ {\vf}_{U,i} $ maps $ U \times \fs{F} $
into $ \fs{F} $ with $ {\vf}_{U,1}(x,v)=v $. Then, it represents a spray if and only if for
all $ s \in \rr $ we have
\begin{equation}\label{eq:1}
{\vf}_{U,2}(x,sv)=s^2{\vf}_{U,2}(x,v).
\end{equation}
Thus, we see that Condition \eqref{eq:sp1} (in addition to being a second-order vector field), 
simply means that $ {\vf}_{U,2} $ is homogeneous of degree 2 in the
variable $ v $.  It follows that $ {\vf}_{U,2} $ is a quadratic
map in its second variable, and specifically, this quadratic map is given by
\begin{equation*}
{\vf}_{U,2} (x,v) = \dfrac{1}{2}\dd_2^2 {\vf}_{U,2} (x, 0)(v,v).
\end{equation*}
Thus the spray is induced by a symmetric bilinear map given at each point
x in a chart by
\begin{equation}\label{eq:sp3}
\mathbb{B}= \dfrac{1}{2}\dd_2^2 {\vf}_{U,2} (x, 0),
\end{equation}
here $ \dd_2^2 $ is the second partial derivative with respect to the second variable.
Conversely, suppose given a map $ x \in U \mapsto \mathbb{B}(x) $, where $ \mathbb{B}(x) $ is a symmetric bilinear $ C^{k-1} $-map from  $\fs{F} \times \fs{F}$ to $\fs{F} $.
Thus, for
each $ v, w \in \fs{E} $ the value of $  \mathbb{B}(x) $ at $ (v, w) $ is denoted by $ \mathbb{B}(x; v, w) $ or
$ \mathbb{B}(x)(v, w) $. Define $ {\vf}_{U,2}(x, v) = \mathbb{B}(x; v,v) $. Then $ {\vf}_{U,2} $ is quadratic in its
second variable, and a $ C^k $-spray over $ U $ can be represented by the map $ {\vf}_{U,2} $ defined by
\begin{equation*}
{\vf}_{U,2}(x, v) = (v, (\mathbb{B}(x; t),t)) = (v, {\vf}_{U,2}(x, v)).
\end{equation*}
The mapping $ \mathbb{B} $ is called the symmetric bilinear map 
associated with the spray.

 Let $  \mathsf{V}^{k}(\fs{M}), \mathcal{E}^k(\fs{M}) $  be the sets of all $C^{k}$-vector fields and
$C^k$-real-valued maps on $ \fs{M} $ respectively, $X,Y \in \mathsf{V}^{k}(\fs{M})$, and $ [X,Y] $ the bracket product.  A covariant derivative $ \nabla $
 is an $\rr$-bilinear map: 
 \begin{equation*}
 \begin{array}{cccc}
 \nabla : \mathsf{V}^{k}(\fs{M}) \times \mathsf{V}^{k}(\fs{M}) \to \mathsf{V}^{k}(\fs{M}),\quad
 (X,Y) \to \nabla_XY
 \end{array}
 \end{equation*} 
 such that for all $ \varphi \in \mathcal{E}^k(\fs{M})$ and $ X,Y \in \mathsf{V}^{k}(\fs{M}) $ the following hold: 
 \begin{enumerate}[label=$ \bl{(CD.\arabic*)} $,ref=CD.\arabic*]
 	\item \label{eq:cd2}$ \nabla_{\varphi X}Y = \varphi \nabla_{ X}Y$,
 	\item \label{eq:cd3}$\nabla_{ X}(\varphi Y) = (\mathcal{L}_{X}\varphi)Y + \varphi \nabla_{ X}Y$,
 	\item \label{eq:cd4}$\nabla_XY - \nabla_YX = [X,Y]$.
 \end{enumerate}

 Given a spray $ \s $  on $ \fs{M} $, then there exists a unique covariant
derivative $ \nabla $ (of class $ C^{k-1} $) such that in a chart $ U $, the derivative is given by 
\begin{equation}\label{eq:cd1}
(\nabla_XY)_U(x) = Y'_U(x)X_U(x)-\mathbb{B}_U(x)(X_U(x),Y_U(x)),
\end{equation}
see \cite[p. 5]{k3}. Condition \ref{eq:cd4} is added for the existence of  bijective correspondence between sprays and covariant derivative, \cite[VIII, $ \S 2 $ ]{lang}.
\section{Associated Connection Maps}
Let $ \pr_{\fs{M}}: T\fs{M} \to \fs{M} $ and $ \pr_{T\fs{M}}: T(T\fs{M}) \to T\fs{M} $ be the tangent and double tangent bundles respectively. Let $(U,\phi )$ be a local trivialization for $ \fs{M} $, $  \phi(U) \times \fs{F} $ the corresponding chart for
$ T\fs{M} $, and $ (\phi(U) \times \fs{F}) \times (\fs{F} \times \fs{F}) $  the corresponding chart for $ T(T\fs{M}) $.
Let $ \pr_{\fs{M}}^*T\fs{M} $ be the pullback bundle induced by $ \pr_{\fs{M}} $, 
$ \pr_{\fs{M}}^* : \pr_{\fs{M}}^*T\fs{M} \to T\fs{M}$  its projection, and $ (\phi(U) \times \fs{F}) \times \fs{F} $ 
the corresponding chart for $ \pr_{\fs{M}}^*T\fs{M} $.

In charts, the tangent map $ T\pr_{T\fs{M}} $ has the form: $$ (\phi{(U)} \times \fs{F}) \times (\fs{F} \times \fs{F}) \to  \phi{(U)} \times \fs{F}, \quad T\pr_{T\fs{M}}(u,x,y,z) = (u,y),$$
and we have the following commutative diagram: 
\begin{equation} \label{dig:1}
\begin{tikzcd}
{(\phi{(U)}\times\mathbb{F})\times (\mathbb{F} \times \mathbb{F})} && {\phi{(U)}\times\mathbb{F}} \\
{\phi{(U)}\times \mathbb{F}} && \phi{(U)}
\arrow["{T\mathrm{Pr}_{\mathbb{M}}}", from=1-1, to=1-3]
\arrow["{\mathrm{Pr}_{\mathbb{M}}}", from=1-3, to=2-3]
\arrow["{\mathrm{Pr}_{\mathbb{M}}}", from=2-1, to=2-3]

\end{equation}

Define the mapping $ \mathcal{H}: T(T\fs{M}) \to \pr_{T\fs{M}}^*T\fs{M} $ so that $ T\pr_{T\fs{M}} = \pr_{\fs{M}}^* \circ \mathcal{H} $. Then Diagram \eqref{dig:1} leads to the  diagram:

\end{tikzcd}
\end{equation}
with $ \mathcal{H}(u,x,y,z) = (u,x,y)$ and $ \pr_{\fs{M}}^*(u,y,z)=(u,z) $. Let
$ \overline{\mathcal{H}} = \pr_{\fs{M}}^* \circ \mathcal{H}$, then $ \overline{\mathcal{H}} =T\pr_{T\fs{M}} $.

Let $ V $ be another chart and $ \va : U \to V $ an isomorphism. For a given spray $ \s $, we will need a transformation rule for a change  of charts under  $ \va $. Locally, the map $ T\va $ has the form:
\begin{equation*}
\upphi: \phi({U}) \times \fs{F}  \to \fs{F} \times \fs{F}, \quad \upphi(x,v)=(\va(x),\va'(x)v).
\end{equation*}
Then, the change of chart for $ TT\va $ is given  by
\begin{gather*}
\widehat{\upphi}: \phi{(U)} \times \fs{F} \times \fs{F} \times \fs{F} \to \phi(V) \times \fs{F} \times \fs{F} \times \fs{F}, \quad \widehat{\upphi}=(\upphi, \upphi').
\end{gather*}
The derivative  $ \upphi' $ is given as follows:
\[
\upphi'(x,v)= \begin{bmatrix}
\va'(x) & 0 \\
\va''(x)v & \va'(x)
\end{bmatrix}\mathbf{v}, \quad
\mathbf{v}=\begin{bmatrix} u \\ v \end{bmatrix}, \; u,v \in \fs{F}.
\]
Thus,
\begin{equation}
\widehat{\upphi}(x, v, u, w) \mapsto \big(\va(x), \va'(x)u, \va'(x)v, \va''(x)(u,v) + \va'(x)w \big).
\end{equation}
As mentioned in Section \ref{sec:1}, in a chart $ U $, we can write the local representative of $ \s $ as $ {\s_U}=({\s_{U,1}}, {\s_{U,2}}) $ with $ {\s_{U,1}}(x,v)=v  $, and $ {\s_{U,2}} $ satisfies Equation \eqref{eq:1}.
Likewise in a chart $ V $, the local representatives of $ \s $ is ${\s_V}=({\s_{V,1}}, {\s_{V,2}}) $. These representatives satisfy the following equation:
\begin{equation}
\s_V(\va(x),\va'(x)v) = \big(\va'(x)v, \va''(x)(v,v)+\va'(x)\s_{U,2}(x,v)\big),
\end{equation} 
because $ w=  \s_{U,2}(x,v)$, and $ \s_{U,1}(x,v)=\s_{V,1}(x,v)= v $ so we may put $ u=v $. Thus,
\begin{gather}\label{gat:1}\nonumber
\s_{V,2}\big(\va(x), \va'(x)v \big) = \va''(x)(v, v)+ h'(x)\s_{U,2}(x,v), \\ 
\mathbb{B}_U\big(\va(x);\va'(x)v,\va'(x)w \big)=\va''(x)(v,w)+\va'(x)\mathbb{B}_V\big(x;v,w \big). 
\end{gather}
\begin{lemma}\label{lem:1}
	Let $ \s $ be a given spray on $ \fs{M} $, and $ \fs{B} $ the symmetric bilinear map associated with $ \s $.
	Then, there exists a unique vector bundle morphism $ \mathcal{L}: T(TM) \to \pr_{\fs{M}}^*T\fs{M}$
	over $ T\fs{M} $
	such that over a chart $ U $, we get
	\begin{equation}
	\mathcal{L}_U(x,v,u,w) = (x,v,w-\fs{B}_U(x;v,u)).
	\end{equation} 
	Furthermore, there exists a unique vector bundle morphism $ \overline{\mathcal{L}} $ such that
	\begin{equation}\label{eq:3}
\overline{\mathcal{L}}_U(x,v,u,w) = (x,w-\fs{B}_U(x;v,u)).
\end{equation} 
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
Let $ V $ be another chart and $ \va : U \to V $ an isomorphism. 
As discussed above, the change of chart for $ (TT\va)_U $ is given  by
\begin{gather*}
\widehat{\upphi}: \phi(U) \times \fs{F} \times \fs{F} \times \fs{F} \to \phi(U) \times \fs{F} \times \fs{F} \times \fs{F}\\  \widehat{\upphi}(x, v, u, w) \mapsto \big(\va(x), \va'(x)u, \va'(x)v, \va''(x)(u,v) + \va'(x)w \big).
\end{gather*}
Thus,
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_V \circ \widehat{\upphi}(x, v, u, w) =\big(\va(x), \va'(x)u, \va'(x)w \big).
\end{equation}
Because $ \va''(x)(u,v) $ drops in the fourth coordinates. Therefore, the family of mappings $ \set{\mathcal{L}_U} $
defines a vector bundle morphism over $ T\fs{M} $, and its local expression reveals that it is indeed a vector bundle isomorphism over $ U $. This concludes the proof of the first part. 
To prove the second part let $ \overline{\mathcal{L}} = \pr_{\fs{M}}^* \circ \mathcal{L} $. Then,
it is a vector bundle morphism satisfying \eqref{eq:3}.
\end{proof}
\begin{theorem}\label{th:con}
Let $ \s $ be a given spray on $ \fs{M} $, and $ \nabla $ its associated covariant derivative.
Then there exists a unique vector bundle morphism 
\begin{equation}
K : T(T\fs{M}) \to T\fs{M}
\end{equation} 
such that $ \nabla = K \circ T $, and for all $ C^{k-1} $-vector fields
$ X,Y $ on $ \fs{M} $ the following diagram is commutative:
\[\begin{tikzcd}
{T\mathbb{M}} & {} & {T(T\mathbb{M})} \\
{\mathbb{M}} && {T\mathbb{M}}
\arrow["TX", from=1-1, to=1-3]
\arrow["K", from=1-3, to=2-3]
\arrow["{\nabla_YX}", from=2-1, to=2-3]
\arrow["Y"', from=2-1, to=1-1]
\end{tikzcd}\]
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
The morphism that we are looking for is actually $ \overline{\mathcal{L}} $ whose existence and uniqueness was proved in Lemma \ref{lem:1}. In a chart $ U $, $ K = \overline{\mathcal{L}} $ has the form:
\begin{gather*}
K_U(x,v): \fs{F} \times \fs{F} \to \fs{F}\\
(u,w) \mapsto w-\fs{B}_U(x;v,u)
\end{gather*}
that fulfills the conditions of the theorem. 
\end{proof}
The expression $ \nabla = K \circ T  $ indicates that $ K $ is  a connection map in the sense of Eliasson \cite{eli} and Vilms \cite{vilms}. We call it a connection map associated with the spray. This connection map is fully characterized by the associated bilinear map $ \fs{B} $. The differentiability of the spray implies that $ K $ is of class $ C^{k-1} $. 
The linearity of $ \fs{B} $ with respect to the second variable implies that $ K $ is linear on the fibers of $ T(T\fs{M}) $, and therefore $ K $ is a linear connection map. Also, the  connection map $ K $ is symmetric in variables $ u $ and $ v $  because $ \fs{B} $ is symmetric. 
\begin{remk}
We define a connection map by means of the associated covariant of a spray. A question that way may ask is if a connection map determines a spray. In fact, the question is if a covariant derivative determines a spray. In  \cite[VIII, $ \S 2 $ ]{lang}, it was pointed out if a manifold admits a cutoff function and we use the torsion-free covariant derivative which yields the existence of the symmetric bilinear mapping $ \fs{B}_U $ for each chart $ U $, then there exists a bijection between sprays and covariant derivative. Therefore, in this case a connection map  determines a spray. If a \fr manifold admits a smooth partition of unity (in particular nuclear \fr manifolds) then there exists a cutoff function on the manifold.   
\end{remk}
Next, because of the importance of second-order tangent bundles (see \cite{a,dod3}) and the possibility of handling them by our approach we will study them with details. First, we show that a connection map induces a vector bundle structure on the second-order tangent bundle $ T^2\fs{M} $.

The second-order tangent bundle $ T^2\fs{M} $ is the set of all 2-jets of $ \fs{M} $.
The space $ T^2\fs{M} $ has the fiber bundle structure over $ \fs{M} $, its bundle projection $ \pr^2_{\fs{M}} : T^2\fs{M} \to \fs{M}$ is defined by $ \pr_{\fs{M}}^2(j^2_x(\gamma))=x$, where  $ \gamma : \rr \to \fs{M} $
is mapping such that $ \gamma(0)=x $. However, it is well known that, in general, $ T^2\fs{M} $ is not a vector bundle.

Let $ \fs{N} $ be a $ C^k $-\fr manifold,  and $ \va : \fs{M} \to \fs{N} $ be a $ C^k $-mapping, $ k\geq 1 $.
The second-order tangent map $ T^2\va = T^2M \to T^2N $ is defined by $ j^2_x\gamma \mapsto j^2_{\va(x)} (\va \circ \gamma) $. 

Despite of being a fiber bundle morphism, $ T^2\va $ is not always linear on the fibers of $ T^2\fs{M} $
and $ T^2\fs{N} $ because it involves the second order derivatives.
Now, we show that the associated connection map of a spray induces a vector bundle structure on $ \fs{M} $.
\begin{theorem}
Let $ \s $ be a given spray on $ \fs{M} $, and $ K $ its associated connection map. Then $K$
induces a vector bundle structure on $ T^2\fs{M} $ over $ \fs{M} $, and gives rise to the  isomorphism of this vector bundle with the vector bundle $ T\fs{M}\oplus  T\fs{M}$.
\end{theorem} 
\begin{proof}
The proof is identical to the one in \cite[Theorem 4.1]{k1}. Just, in our setting, we need to consider  the following mapping:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:iso}
\Upsilon \coloneq (\pr_{T\fs{M}}, \overline{\mathcal{H}},  K) : T(T\fs{M}) \to T\fs{M} \oplus T\fs{M} \oplus T\fs{M}
\end{equation}
to obtain an isomorphism of fiber bundles over $ \fs{M} $.
\end{proof}

In \cite{dod}, it was observed that the vector bundle structure induced on $ T^2\fs{M} $ depends on the choice of a connection map. However, it was proved that this vector bundle structure remains invariant  under conjugate connection maps with respect to diffeomorphisms of a manifold. We have the same situation. For the sake of completes we discuss this matter just briefly because the arguments are almost the same. 

We will adopt the notion of conjugacy of connection maps introduced in \cite{vas}. Let
$ \s_1 $ and $ \s_2 $ be sprays on $ \fs{M} $, and let $ K_1 $ and $ K_2 $ be their associated connection maps respectively. Let $ \upmu $ be a diffeomorphism of $ \fs{M} $. Then $ K_1, K_2 $ are called 
$ \upmu $-conjugate (or $\upmu$-related) if
\begin{equation}\label{eq:conj}
T\upmu \circ K_1 = K_2 \circ T(T \upmu).
\end{equation}
Let $ (U,\phi) $ and $ (V,\psi) $ be local trivializations of $ \fs{M} $, and $ \upmu_{UV} \coloneq \psi \circ \upmu \circ \phi^{-1} $ the local representation of $ T^2\upmu $. Then, locally Equation \eqref{eq:conj} becomes:
\begin{gather}\label{eq:conjloc}
\dd \upmu_{UV}(\phi(x))\big(-\fs{B}_U(\phi(x))(u,u) \big) = \\ \nonumber
\dd^2 \uptau_{UV}\big(\phi(x)\big)(u,u)-\fs{B}_V \big(\upmu_{UV}(\phi(x)) \big) \Big(\dd \upmu_{UV}(\phi(x))(u), \dd \upmu_{UV}(\phi(x))(u)\Big),
\end{gather}
for every $ (x,u)  \in U \times \fs{F}$.
To obtain Expression \eqref{eq:conjloc} we need the local representations of $ \upmu $ and its corresponding tangent map $ T\upmu $, also the local representations of the connection maps. The detailed computations can be found in 
\cite[$ \S 1.5.6 $]{dod2} and we will skip them. A point that we need to take into account is that in 
\cite[$ \S 1.5.6 $]{dod2} the local representations of connections maps are given in terms of Christoffel symbols $ \Gamma $, and we need to use the equality $ \Gamma(x)(u,v) = - \fs{B}(x)(u,v) $ instead. Again, by using the same computations as in \cite[$ \S 8.3 $]{dod2} we find the  local representation of $ T^2\upmu $ as follows:
define the mapping  
\begin{gather*}
\lambda_{U}: \pr_{\fs{M}}^2(U) \to U \times \fs{F} \times \fs{F} \\
\lambda_{U} (j^2_x \gamma) = \Big(x, (\phi \circ \gamma)'(0), (\phi \circ \gamma)''(0) 
-\fs{B}(\phi(x)) \big( (\phi \circ \gamma)'(0), (\phi \circ \gamma)'(0)\big)
\Big),
\end{gather*}
for every $ x \in  (\pr_{\fs{M}}^2)^{-1}(U)$. Then $ (U, \phi, \Phi_U^2) $ is the local trivializations of
$ T^2\fs{M} $. Here, the diffeomorphism $ \Phi_U^2: (\pr_{\fs{M}}^2)^{-1}(U) \to \phi(U) \times \fs{F} \times \fs{F} $ is defined by
$$
\Phi_U^2 \coloneq (\phi \times \id_{\fs{F}} \times \id_{\fs{F}}) \circ \lambda_U.
$$
Then the local representation turns into:  
\begin{gather}\label{eq:find}
\Big (\Phi^2_{V, \upmu(x)} \circ T^2_x \upmu \circ (\Phi^2_{U, (x)})^{-1} \Big)(h,k) = \\ \nonumber
= \Big(\dd \upmu_{UV}(y)(h), \dd \upmu_{UV}(y)(k)+ \dd \upmu_{UV}(y)\fs{B}_U(y)(h,h)
+ \dd^2 \upmu_{UV}(y)(h,h)- \\ \nonumber
-\fs{B}_V (\upmu_{UV}(y)) \big( \dd \upmu_{UV}(y)(h), \dd \upmu_{UV}(y)(h)
\big)
\Big),
\end{gather}
for every $ (y,h,k) \in \phi(U) \times \fs{F} \times \fs{F} $ and $ x = \phi^{-1}(y) $. Now, if $ K_1 $ and
$ K_2 $ are $ \upmu $-conjugated, then by combining \eqref{eq:find} and \eqref{eq:conjloc} we get:
\begin{gather}\label{eq:fin}
\Big (\Phi^2_{V, \upmu(x)} \circ T^2_x \upmu \circ (\Phi^2_{U, (x)})^{-1} \Big)(h,k) = \\ \nonumber
= \big( \dd \upmu_{UV}(\phi(x))(h), \dd \upmu_{UV}(\phi(x))(k)
\big)
\end{gather}
which implies that $ T^2\upmu : T^2 \fs{M} \to T^2\fs{M} $ is linear. Moreover, it implies that
$$
x \mapsto \Phi^2_{V, \upmu(x)} \circ T^2_x \upmu \circ (\Phi^2_{U, (x)})^{-1}
$$
is differentiable, therefore, $ (T^2\upmu, \upmu) $ is a vector bundle isomorphism.
Thus, the vector bundle structures induced by $ K_1 $ and $ K_2 $ on $ T^2\fs{M} $ are isomorphic.
\section{Associated Linear Symmetric Connection}
The following notion of nonlinear connection was introduced by Barthel \cite{ba}, then studied by Vilms \cite{vilms} for Banach
manifolds. We will apply this notion for \fr manifolds too.  

In this section, for the sake of simplicity we assume that  $\fs{M}$ is a  smooth Fr\'{e}chet manifold. 
  Let $ \pr_{\fs{M}}: T\fs{M} \to \fs{M} $ be  tangent bundle, $ \pr^*_{\fs{M}} T\fs{M}$  the pullback bundle induced by $ \pr_{\fs{M}} $.
  
   Define the mapping 
  \begin{gather}
  \pr_{\fs{M}}!: T(T\fs{M}) \to \pr_{\fs{M}}^*T\fs{M} \\ \nonumber
    \pr_{\fs{M}}^*(v_x)=(x, T\pr_{\fs{M},x}(v_x), \quad \forall v_x \in T_x(T\fs{M})).
  \end{gather}
  The mapping $ \pr_{\fs{M}}! $ is smooth and onto with 
  $ \ker \pr_{\fs{M}}! = \ker T\pr_{\fs{M}} = \mathbf{V}T\fs{M}$ (the vertical subbundle of $ T(T\fs{M}) $).
  
 Consequently, we have the following exact sequence of vector bundles over $ T\fs{M} $:
 \begin{equation}\label{eq:exact}
 0 \to \mathbf{V}T\fs{M} \xrightarrow{\eu{I}} T(T\fs{M})\xrightarrow{\pr_{\fs{M}}!} \pr^*_{\fs{M}} T\fs{M} \to 0,
 \end{equation}
 where $ \eu{I} $ is the canonical inclusion. A smooth nonlinear connection on $ T\fs{M} $ is a splitting $ \eu{C} $ on the left
 of the exact sequence \eqref{eq:exact}. It means that $ \eu{C} : T(T\fs{M}) \to \mathbf{V}T\fs{M}$ is a mapping such that
 $ \eu{C} \circ \eu{I} =\Id_{\mathbf{V}T\fs{M}} $. The horizontal bundle $ \mathbf{H}T\fs{M}$ is defined as the kernel $\ker \eu{C},  \mathbf{H}T\fs{M} = \ker \eu{C}$. It is well known that in a presence of a connection $ \eu{C}$ we have $ T(T\fs{M})= \mathbf{H}T\fs{M} \oplus \mathbf{V}T\fs{M} $, and the existence of s subbundle $ \mathbf{H}T\fs{M} $ satisfying
 $ \mathbf{H}T\fs{M} \oplus \mathbf{V}T\fs{M} $ implies the existence of a nonlinear connection in $ T\fs{M} $.
 
 A connection  $ \eu{C} $ such that additionally  $ \eu{C}: T(T\fs{M}) \to \mathbf{V}T\fs{M} \to T(T\fs{M}) $
 is $ (T\pr_{\fs{M}},T\pr_{\fs{M}}) $ fiberwise linear is called a linear connection. Next, we will define symmetric connections.
 
 It is well known that $ T(T\fs{M}) $ has two vector bundle structure as a bundle over $ T(\fs{M}) $, namely the structure induced by $ T\pr_{T\fs{M}} $, and the secondary structure with  $ T(\fs{M}) $ as the base manifold. There is a canonical involution $ \mathtt{Inv} : T(T\fs{M}) \to  T(\fs{M})$ that interchanges two structures. 
 In \cite[Theorem 1]{sym}, it was proved that for finite dimensional manifolds $ \mathtt{Inv} $ is a 
 $ T(\fs{M}) $ isomorphism between the primarily and the secondary vector bundle structures on $ T(\fs{M}) $
 such that the following diagram commutes:
\[\begin{tikzcd}
{T(T(\mathbb{M}))} & {} & {T(T(\mathbb{M}))} \\
{T(\mathbb{M})} & {} & {T(\mathbb{M})}
\arrow["{\mathtt{Inv}}", from=1-1, to=1-3]
\arrow["{T\Pi_{\mathbb{M}}}", from=1-3, to=2-3]
\arrow["{\mathrm{Id}}", from=2-1, to=2-3]
\arrow["{\Pi_{T\mathbb{M}}}"', from=1-1, to=2-1]
\end{tikzcd}\]
 The mapping  $ \mathtt{Inv} $ equals its inverse and its fixed points are exactly the symmetric vectors ($ v \in T(T\fs{M}) $ is called symmetric if $ T\pr_{T\fs{M}}(v) = \pr_{T\fs{M}}(v) $).  
 The statements of \cite[Theorem 1]{sym} also remains valid for infinite dimensional manifolds.
 A connection $ \eu{C} $ is called linear symmetric if $ \eu{C} = \eu{C} \circ \mathtt{Inv}$.

  One of the important problems concerning the notion of linear symmetric
 connection consists in its determinations from a spray. Next, we prove that a spray induces a unique linear symmetric connection and vice versa.
 \begin{theorem}\label{th:ch}
 Suppose $\s  $ is spray on $ \fs{M} $. Then there exists a unique linear symmetric connection on $ T\fs{M} $ which is fully characterized
 by the associated symmetric bilinear mappings of $ \s $. Conversely, if $ \eu{C} $ is a linear symmetric connection on
 $ T\fs{M} $, then there exists a unique spray on $ \fs{M} $ whose associated connection map is determined by $ \eu{C} $.  
 \end{theorem}
 \begin{proof}
 Let $ K: T(T\fs{M}) \to \fs{M} $ be the associated connection map of $ \s $.  Let $ U $ be a chart of $ \fs{M}$, $ \fs{B}_U $  associated symmetric bilinear over $ U $, $ K_U(x,v,u,w) = (x,w-\fs{B}_U(x;v,u))$ the local representation of $ K $. Define locally the mapping: 
 \begin{gather}
  \eu{K}: \mathbf{V}T\fs{M} \to T\fs{M},\\ \nonumber
  \eu{K}(x,u,0,v)= (x,v), \quad \forall (x,v) \in U \times \fs{F}.
 \end{gather}
 Since $ K $ is a smooth bundle morphism, taking account the definition of $\eu{K}$, it follows that $ K $ 
 factors uniquely into $ K= \eu{K} \circ \eu{C} $, where $ \eu{C} : T(T\fs{M}) \to \mathbf{V}(T\fs{M}) $
 is a smooth morphism which is locally given by
 \begin{equation*}
 \eu{C}(x,u,v,w)=(x,u,0,w-\fs{B}_U(x)(v,u))).
 \end{equation*}
Now, let $  v= 0$, thus $ \eu{C} \circ \eu{I} = \Id_{\mathbf{V}T\fs{M}} $, which means $ \eu{C} $ is a connection.
The linearity of $ \fs{B}_U $ with respect to the second variables implies that $\eu{C} $ is linear on the fibers of $ T(T\fs{M}) $, and therefore $ \eu{C} $ is linear. Also, since $ \fs{B}_U $ is symmetric we have
\begin{equation*}
\eu{C}(x,u,v,w) = \eu{C}(x,v,u,w)
\end{equation*}
which means $ \eu{C} \circ \mathtt{Inv} = \eu{C} $,  because the mapping $ \mathtt{Inv} $ switches the middle coordinates.
Thus, $ \eu{C} $ is symmetric. 

Conversely, by definition $ \eu{C} $ is the left splitting of the exact sequence \eqref{eq:exact} such that
 $ \eu{C} \circ \eu{I} =\Id_{\mathbf{V}T\fs{M}} $.  
 %Define $ \eu{C}_1 :  T(T\fs{M}) \to \mathbf{V}T\fs{M}$
% the morphism on fibers as identity on element of $ \mathbf{V}_uT\fs{M} $ (vertical vectors) and zero on elements of
%  $ \mathbf{H}_uT\fs{M} $ (horizontal vectors). Thus, $ \eu{C}_1 $ is the left splitting of the exact sequence \eqref{eq:exact}, i.e., $ \eu{C}_1 \circ \eu{I} = \Id_{\mathbf{V}T\fs{M}}$.
 Let $ \mathrm{Pr}_2 $ be the projection on second factor of $ T\fs{M} \times T\fs{M} $. Now, by using the well known identification $ \Bbbk: \mathbf{V}T\fs{M} \simeq T\fs{M} \times  T\fs{M}$ we can find a mapping
 $ \eu{C}_2 :  \mathbf{V}T\fs{M} \to T\fs{M} $ with $ \eu{C}_2 = \mathrm{Pr}_2 \circ \Bbbk   $ so that
 $ (\eu{C}_2, \pr_{\fs{M}}) $ is a vector morphism between $ \mathbf{V}T\fs{M} $ and $ T\fs{M} $.
 
 Define a mapping $ K \coloneq \eu{C}_2 \circ \eu{C} : T(T\fs{M}) \to T\fs{M} $. It is evident that
 $ v \in T\fs{M} $ is horizontal ($ v $ belongs to to the horizontal space $ \mathbf{H}T\fs{M} $)
 if and only if
 \begin{equation}\label{eq:cm}
 \ker K = \mathbf{H}T\fs{M}.
 \end{equation}
 We will find the local representation of $ K $. Let $ (U,\phi) $ be a local trivialization of $ \fs{M} $.
  The exact sequence $ \eqref{eq:exact} $ locally turns into
  \begin{equation*}
  0 \to \phi(U) \times \fs{F} \times \set{0} \times \fs{F} \to \phi(U) \times \fs{F} \times \fs{F} \times \fs{F} \to
\phi(U) \times \fs{F} \times \set{0} \times \fs{F} \to 0,
  \end{equation*}
  so that $ \eu{I}: (x,v,0,w) \mapsto  (x,v,0,w)  $,  $ \pr_{\fs{M}}! (x,u,v) \mapsto (x,u,w) $, and
  $ \Bbbk : (x,u,0,w) \mapsto (x,u,w) $. Locally, $ \eu{C} $ maps $ (x,u,v,w)$ to $(x,u,0, \eu{C}_U(x,u,v,w)) $,
  where $ \eu{C}_U $ is symmetric in $ u  $ and $ v $ and linear in $ u, v $ and $ w $.
  
  The equality $ \eu{C} \circ \eu{I} = \Id_{\mathbf{V}T\fs{M}} $ implies that $ \eu{C}_U(x,u,0,w) =w $. Now, define
  $$ \widehat{\eu{C}}_U \coloneq \eu{C}(x,u,v,w)+w $$ which does not depend on $ w $ and
  \begin{equation*}
  \widehat{\eu{C}}_U(x,u,v,w_1)+\widehat{\eu{C}}_U(x,u,v,w_2) = \widehat{\eu{C}}_U(x,u,v,w_1+w_2).
  \end{equation*}
  
 %Let $(U, \phi_U)  $ be a local trivialization for $ \fs{M} $,
 % $ (\phi_U(U) \times \fs{F}, \overline{\phi}_U) $
 %the corresponding chart for $ T(\fs{M}) $, and  $ (\phi_U(U) \times \fs{F} \times \fs{F} \times \fs{F}, \widehat{\phi}_U) $  the corresponding chart for $ T(T(\fs{M})) $. Here,
 %$ \overline{\phi}_U = (\phi_U \times \id_{\fs{F}}) \circ \upphi_U (\text{tanstion function}) : \pr^{-1}_{T\fs{M}}(U) %\to  \phi_U(U) \times \fs{F} $. Also,
% \begin{gather*}
% \widehat{\phi}_U= \upphi^{-1}_{T\fs{M}}(\pr^{-1}_{T\fs{M}}) \to \phi_U(U) \times \fs{F} \times \fs{F} \times \fs{F} \\
% j^2_u \gamma \mapsto (\phi_U(u), (\phi_U \circ \gamma)'(0)),
%  \end{gather*}
Let $ K_U  $ be the local representation of $ K $ over $ U $. Then
\begin{gather*}
K_U(x,u,v,w) = (\mathrm{Pr}_2 \circ \Bbbk )(x,u,0,\eu{C}_U(x,u,v,w)) = (x, \eu{C}_U(x,u,v,w)).
\end{gather*}
But $ \eu{C}_U(x,u,v,w) = w - \widehat{\eu{C}}_U(x,u,v)  $, where $ \widehat{\eu{C}}_U $
is symmetric and linear in $ u $ and $ v $. Thus, we can write
\begin{equation*}
K_U(x,u,v,w) = (x,w- \fs{B}_U(x)(u,v))
\end{equation*}
where $ \fs{B}_U : \phi_U(U) \times \fs{F}  \times \fs{F} \to  \fs{F} $
is a smooth map, and  symmetric and linear in $ u $ and $ v $.
This means $ K $ is a connection map, and $ \fs{B}_U  $ induces a spray whose connection map is $ K $.
 \end{proof}

Next, we show that any linear connection  on $ T\fs{M} $ induces a linear connection on $ T^2\fs{M} $ and vice versa.
To achieve that we need the following lemma which for finite-dimensional manifolds was proved in \cite{duc}, and the proof is almost identical.  

Let $ \fs{V}_1 $ and $ \fs{V}_2 $ be smooth vector bundles over $ \fs{M} $, and $ \va: \fs{M} \to \fs{M} $ a $ C^1 $-mapping. A mapping $ \upphi : \fs{V}_1 \to \fs{V}_2 $ is called a $ \va $-morphism if for any 
$ x \in \fs{M} $, there exists a vector bundle chart $ (U, \psi) $ of $ \fs{V}_1 $ at $ x $, a vector bundle chart
$ (V, \uppsi) $ of $ \fs{V}_2 $ at $ \va{(x)} $, and a $ C^1 $-mapping $ \Phi_{VU} : (U \cap \va^{-1}(U)) \times \fs{F} \to \fs{F} $ such that 
$$ \upphi_x \circ \psi^{-1}  = \uppsi^{-1}_{\va(x)} \circ \Phi_{VU}, \quad \forall x \in U \cap \va^{-1}(V).$$
\begin{lemma}\label{lem:2}
Let $ \upphi: \fs{V}_1 \to \fs{V}_2  $ be a $ \va $-morphism which is isomorphism on fibers. Then, 
$ T_u \fs{V}_1 \simeq T_{\upphi{(u)}} \fs{V}_2 $ for any $ u \in U $ (a chart for $ \fs{M} $). Furthermore, 
any (linear symmetric) connection on $ \fs{V}_2 $ induces a (linear symmetric) connection on $ \fs{V}_2 $.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
From the identity $ T\pr_{\fs{V}_2} \circ T \phi = T \va \, \circ T \pr_{\fs{V}_2} $ and the assumption that $ \phi $ is an isomorphism on fibers, it follows that $ T_u \fs{V}_1 \simeq T_{\upphi{(u)}} \fs{V}_2 $ for any $ u \in U $.
Now, let $ \eu{C}_2 $ be the left splitting that determines the (linear) connection on $ \fs{V}_2 $. Let
$$ \eu{C}_1 \coloneq (T\phi\mid_{\mathbf{V}\fs{V}_1})^{-1} \circ \eu{C}_2 \circ T \phi ,$$
 then it is a left splitting
for the exact sequence associated to $ \fs{V}_1 $, i.e., a (linear) connection on $ \fs{V}_1 $. 
\end{proof}
\begin{theorem}\label{th:ind}
Suppose $ \fs{M} $ is given a spray $ \s $. Any linear symmetric connection on $ T\fs{M} $ induces a linear symmetric connection on the vector bundle $ T^2\fs{M} $, and vice versa.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
	The spray $ \s $ induces a vector bundle structure on $ T^2\fs{M} $. Let $ K $ be the associated connection map of $ \s $. 

Suppose that  $ \eu{C} $ is a linear symmetric connection on $ T\fs{M} $.
	The restriction of $ \eu{C} $ to $ T^2\fs{M} $ ($ T^2 \fs{M} $ is a submanifold of $ T(T\fs{M}) $) is a morphism
	of the vector bundles $ T^2\fs{M} $ and $ T\fs{M} $ which is isomorphism on fibers. Therefore, Lemma
	\ref{lem:2} implies that  $ \eu{C} $ induces a linear symmetric connection on $ T^2\fs{M} $. 
	
	Conversely, let $ \mathcal{C} $ be a linear connection on $ T^2\fs{M} $, and  $ \mathcal{C}_1 \coloneq \mathcal{C} \circ \pr_{T^2\fs{M}} : T( T^2\fs{M}) \to T\fs{M} $. Consider the diffeomorphism $ \Upsilon : T(T\fs{M}) \to 
	 T\fs{M} \oplus T\fs{M} \oplus T\fs{M}$ defined in Equation \eqref{eq:iso} which induces an isomorphism on fibers.
	 Since, $ T^2\fs{M} $ is a submanifold of $ T(T\fs{M}) $ consisting of vectors $ v $ such that 
	 $ \pr_{T\fs{M}}(v) = T\pr_{\fs{M}}(v) $ with $ \Theta : T\fs{M} \oplus T\fs{M} \to T\fs{M} \oplus T\fs{M} \oplus T\fs{M} $, the natural isomorphism  of  $ T\fs{M} \oplus T\fs{M} $ onto
	  $ (\pr_{T\fs{M}} \oplus K \oplus T\pr_{\fs{M}})(T^2\fs{M}) $, then
	 \begin{equation*}
	 \Theta^{-1} \circ (\pr_{T\fs{M}} \oplus K \oplus T\pr_{\fs{M}})(T^2\fs{M}) = T\pr_{\fs{M}} \oplus K (T^2\fs{M}),
	 \end{equation*}
	 and, therefore, $ \mathbf{K} \coloneq T\pr_{\fs{M}} \circ K : T^2\fs{M} \to \fs{M} $ is a diffeomorphism, and determines the vector bundle charts. Let $ \varPi : T\fs{M} \oplus T\fs{M} \to T\fs{M} $ be the projection. Then, $  \varPi\circ \mathbf{K} \circ \mathcal{C}_1  $ gives the morphism of the bundles $ T^2\fs{M} $ and $ T\fs{M} $ 
	 which is an isomorphism on fibers. Therefore, Lemma
	 \ref{lem:2} implies that  $ \mathcal{C} $ induces a connection on $ T\fs{M} $. 
\end{proof}
Next, we characterize nonlinear connections using tangent structures.

	The vertical space $ \mathbf{V}_xT\fs{M} $ at $ v \in T \fs{M} $ is naturally identified with $ T_x\fs{M} $, $ x=\pr_{\fs{M}}(v) $. Define the mapping:
	\begin{gather}\label{eq:verlift}
	\mathtt{Ver}_v: T_x\fs{M} \to \mathbf{V}_vT\fs{M}  \\ \nonumber
	\mathtt{Ver}_v(w) = \derat0 v + t w, \quad  w \in T_x\fs{M}.
	\end{gather}
The mapping $ \mathtt{Ver}_v $ is a linear isomorphism for any $ v \in T\fs{M} $ and is called a vertical lift. Since
$ \ker K = \mathbf{H}T\fs{M} $ (Equation \eqref{eq:cm}), we can easily show that for each $ v \in T_x\fs{M} $ the connection map $ K $ takes $ T_vT\fs{M} $ to $ T_{\pr_{\fs{M}}(v)} \fs{M}$, and satisfies:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:inco}
K (\mathtt{Ver}_v(w)) = w, \quad \forall w \in T_{\pr_{\fs{M}}(v)} \fs{M}.
\end{equation}
Since for $ h \in T\fs{M} $ the mapping $ \Pi_{\mathbb{M}} \mid_{\mathbf{H}_hT\fs{M}} $ is isomorphism and $$ T\pr_{\fs{M},x} : T_hT\fs{M} \to T_x\fs{M} (x =\pr_{T\fs{M}}(h))$$ is an epimorphism then
$ T\pr_{\fs{M},h} \mid_{\mathbf{H}_hT\fs{M}} : \mathbf{H}_hT\fs{M} \to T_x\fs{M} $ is an isomorphism. The inverse of the latter isomorphism $ \mathtt{Hor}_h : T_x\fs{M} \to \mathbf{H}_hT\fs{M} $ is called horizontal lift induced 
by the connection.

Now, define the mapping:
\begin{gather}
\jmath : \pr^*_{T\fs{M}} \to \mathbf{V}T\fs{M} \\ \nonumber
\jmath(x_1,x_2) = \mathsf{Ver}_{x_1}(x_2),\; x_1,x_2 \in T\fs{M},\; \pr_{\fs{M}}(x_1) = \pr_{\fs{M}}(x_2).
\end{gather}
For $ u \in T\fs{M} $, define the mapping $ \eu{J}_u : T_uT\fs{M} \to  \mathbf{V}_uT\fs{M}$ by 
\begin{equation*}
\eu{J}_u \coloneq \eu{I}_u \circ \jmath_u \circ \pr_{\fs{M},u}!.
\end{equation*}
The mapping $ \eu{J} $ (called the tangent structure) can be considered as an $ \mathcal{E}^{\infty}(\fs{M}) $-linear mapping from $ \mathsf{V}(T\fs{M}) \to  \mathsf{V}(T\fs{M})$ defined by $ \eu{J}(V)(u) = \eu{J}_u(V_{\pr_{T\fs{M}}(u)}) $ for $ V \in  \mathsf{V}(T\fs{M}), \; u \in T\fs{M}$. Directly, from the definition it follows that $ \eu{J}^2 =0 $ and 
$ \Img (\eu{J}) = \ker (\eu{J}) =  \mathsf{Sec} (\mathbf{V}T\fs{M}) $ (the space of smooth sections).
The horizontal and vertical lifts are related by 
\begin{equation}\label{eq:impor}
\eu{J}_u \circ \mathtt{Hor}_u = \mathtt{Ver}_u,
\end{equation} 
that is the following diagram is commutative 
\[\begin{tikzcd}
{T_uT\fs{M}} & {} & {T_uT\fs{M}} \\
& {T_{\Pi_\fs{M}(u)}\fs{M}}
\arrow["{\eu{J}_u}", from=1-1, to=1-3]
\arrow["{\mathtt{Ver}_u}", from=1-3, to=2-2]
\arrow["{T\Pi_{\fs{M},u}}"', from=1-1, to=2-2]
\end{tikzcd}\]

Compose from left Equation \eqref{eq:impor} by $K_u $ so $ K_u \circ \eu{J}_u \circ \mathtt{Hor}_u =  K_u \circ\mathtt{Ver}_u  $. By \eqref{eq:inco} the write side is the identity $ \id_{T_{\pr_{\fs{M}}(v)} \fs{M}} $. 
Then, since $ \mathtt{Hor}_u $ is a linear isomorphism, it follows that $ K_u \circ \eu{J}_u $ is its inverse and hence we obtain
\begin{equation}\label{eq:last}
K_u \circ \eu{J}_u = T\pr_{\fs{M},u}.
\end{equation}
Let $ \mathsf{V}(\fs{M}) $ and $ \mathsf{V}(T\fs{M}) $ be the modules of smooth vector fields on $ \fs{M} $ and
$ T\fs{M} $, respectively. Let $ \eu{C} $ be a nonlinear (linear) connection on $ T\fs{M} $. It induces a morphism
$ \widehat{\eu{C}} : \mathsf{V}(\fs{M}) \to \mathsf{Sec} (\mathbf{V}T\fs{M}) $ (the space of smooth sections).
Define the mapping:
\begin{gather}
\mathtt{Vp} : \mathsf{V}(T\fs{M})  \to \mathsf{V}(T\fs{M}) \\ \nonumber
\mathtt{Vp}(v) \coloneq
\begin{cases}
1 & \text{if } \widehat{\eu{C}}(v) \;\text{is vertical}\\
0 & \text{if } v \;\text{is horizontal}.
\end{cases}
\end{gather}
The definition of $ \mathtt{Vp} $ directly implies that $\mathtt{Vp}^2=\mathtt{Vp}  $. Now, define
$ \mathtt{Hp} \coloneq \id_{\mathbf{V}T\fs{M}} -  \mathtt{Vp} $ so that we have $\mathtt{Hp}^2=\mathtt{Hp}  $.
We call $ \mathtt{Vp}  $  ($ \mathtt{Hp}  $) the vertical  (horizontal) projector associated to $ \eu{C} $.
\begin{theorem} \label{th:noncon}
	Any linear symmetric connection on the tangent bundle determines a  connection map and vice versa. 
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Let $ \eu{C} $ be a linear symmetric  connection and $ u \in T\fs{M} $. The mapping
$ \eu{J}_u\mid_{\mathbf{H}_uT\fs{M}}: \mathbf{H}_uT\fs{M} \to \mathbf{V}_uT\fs{M} $ is an isomorphism. Let $ \eu{L}_U $ be its inverse, and define its extension to $ T_uT\fs{M} $ by $ \eu{L}_u \coloneq \eu{L}_u \circ \eu{C}_u $.
Directly, from the definition follows that $ \eu{L}^2 = 0  $ and $ \Img \eu{L} = \ker \eu{L} = \mathbf{H}T\fs{M} $.
Moreover, $ \eu{L} \circ \eu{J} =  \mathtt{Hp}$ and $ \eu{J} \circ \eu{L} = \mathtt{Vp} $. Now define the linear mapping:
\begin{gather*}
K_u : T_uT\fs{M} \to T\pr_{\fs{M}(u)}\fs{M} \\
K_u = T\pr_{\fs{M}(u)} \circ \eu{L}_u.
\end{gather*}
We need to show that $ K_u \circ \eu{J}_u = T\pr_{\fs{M}(u)} $. Since $ \eu{L}_u \circ \eu{J} = (\mathtt{Hp})_u $
and $ T\pr_{\fs{M}(u)} \circ  (\mathtt{Hp})_u = T\pr_{\fs{M}(u)}$ it follows that $ K_u \circ \eu{J}_u = T\pr_{\fs{M}(u)} $.

Conversely, let $ K_u $ be a connection map. Then, $ K_u \circ \eu{J}_u = T\pr_{\fs{M},u}$ (Equation \eqref{eq:last}).
Thus, since $ T\pr_{\fs{M},u} $ is epimorphism, $ K_u  $ is epimorphism too. Let $ \mathbf{H}_u T\fs{M}\coloneq  \ker K_u $
and $ \mathbf{V}_u \coloneq  \ker \eu{J}_U $. From $ K_u \circ \eu{J}_u = T\pr_{\fs{M},u}$  it follows that 
$ \mathbf{H}_u T\fs{M} \cap \mathbf{V}_u T\fs{M} = \set{0}$, and hence $ \mathbf{H}_u T\fs{M} \oplus \mathbf{V}_u T\fs{M} = T_uT\fs{M} $.
\end{proof}
